Can rapid methane emission reduction save us from permafrost thaw?

github.com/hannahjbs/GES

Preprint here

What is the role of methane mitigation in avoiding excessive warming? Methane is a short lived but powerful greenhouse gas. How quickly do we need to reduce our methane emissions? In light of permafrost melt, do we need to reduce our emissions faster?

We use a reduced complexity model of Earth systems to understand the dynamics between methane emissions and long term surface temperature changes.

We model radiative forcing, ocean layer heat transfer, and the carbon cycle, as well as permafrost decomposition. For our projections, we use RCP carbon emissions scenarios while varying methane emission mitigation schemes.

We validate our model against historical records, and it recreates the dynamics we are interested in:

Our model is implemented in python with object-oriented programming, which gives us flexibility to choose what scenario to run. We leverage scipy’s solve_ivp to solve our coupled differiential equations at any given timestep to produce to continuous projections.

Rapid methane mitigation did not prevent a permafrost feedback, and therefore, did not result in a long term temperature reduction. Below shows the linear relationship between the magnitude of methane emissions to temperature, and the negligable impact of rate of reductions.

So, we discovered that final emissions level was the most important factor in determining long-term temperature change. However, further research is needed with more consideration of other methane dynamics.
